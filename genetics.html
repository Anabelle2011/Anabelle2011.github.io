Experiment Process and Analysis

This is the process on how we went through the DNA extraction lab in Mr Sinusas’s 3B class. First, we were introduced to the experiment with DNA models that we used to help us visualize what our DNA looked like. The experiment was all about DNA extraction so it was essential that we understood the basics of what our DNA looks like. We would be using our DNA to see if the section we tested would match with any of our classmates. Before beginning the experiment, we all made predictions about who we might match with and most people predicted that they would match with someone who had a similar race or ethnicity as them. I, along with most others, predicted that ethnicity has something to do with this part of our genetic makeup and that people who shared similar ethnicities would match. Throughout the entire process, we used our sticky note trackers to keep us on track.

As we moved closer to the date of the experiment, we spent a lot of time running mock trials of the experiment which were important to help us get an understanding of what we would be doing in the experiment. We spent some time making a stop motion explainer video of a chosen section. Our section was the way translation works and we animated this process. These trials were important because we only got one chance to do the experiment so we had to work out any errors before we started. It took some time learning how to use the micro pipettes and the tips that go along with it. It was important to have the exact measurements and the micro pipettes helped achieve this. We learned about the VNTR section of our DNA which is one of the most diverse sections of all of our genetic make up. This section can show where everyone may differ. During our experiment, we tested the VNTR section of the DNA to see if people of a similar race would match with each other.

Soon, we started the actual experiment. This involved swabbing our mouths with toothpicks and combining the cells collected with a few solutions that were important to the experiment.We used the micro pipettes to move around the solution. We tapped and flicked the solution to make sure it got thoroughly mixed and then we let it run in an incubator where everyone's DNA got multiplied. After this, we made the gel that would be used for electrophoresis. Everyone inserted their DNA into the gel and then observed as it lit up and moved. Unfortunately our class barely got any results but, luckily the grade data helped us analyze the results. It turned out that there were more matches with different races than within the same race. The number of matches within the same race was 28 while the number of matches within a different race was 242. An example of unlikely matches within the grade was under the category of allele 2 number repeating 18 times. The races of the matches ranged from asian, african american, white and middle eastern.

Throughout the process we took interview videos about our predictions of what would happen and then after the analysis we recorded the results of what had actually happened. I said that I believe that humankind has categorized humans into specific categories based on how they appear. This way of categorizing humans is not biologically correct. One person of a certain race may share more traits with someone of a completely different race than someone of their own race. This proves that although some may look similar their genetics may not match each other as closely (or even at all) as they thought. Based on research done, we have discovered that 99% of humans DNA is exactly the same. Procedure For DNA Extraction Lab

  • Label tubes on the upper sidewall, as writing on the cap or lower sidewall may rub off.
  • Gently scrape the inside of your cheek 3-4 times with a flat-end toothpick.
  • Dip the end of the toothpick with your cheek cells in the X-TractTM buffer. Swirl the toothpick to dislodge the cells. Dispose of the toothpick.
  • Cap your tube.
  • Incubate your tube for 10 minutes at 95°C.
  • Remove your tube from heat and immediately use DNA extract for PCR.
  • Label a PCR tube (200 μl tube) with your initials followed by “P” for PCR
  • Add PCR reagents to your labeled PCR tube. Change pipette tips between samples to prevent contamination.
  • Cap the tubes and ensure the reagents mix well. You may flick each tube with your fingers to ensure proper mixing. Gently tap tubes on your bench to collect liquid at the bottom.
  • Place the tubes inside the miniPCR machine.

Procedure For DNA Extraction Lab (Part 2)

  • Take out the PCR from the incubator and prepare to run it through the gel.
  • Gather your materials for the gel.
  • Set up the Electrodams inside the buffer chamber.
  • Place the comb supports over each side of the buffer chamber.
  • Place the comb in the buffer chamber, resting over the comb supports.
  • Prepare an agarose solution.
  • Mix 30 ml of room temperature 1X TBE buffer and one Agarose Tab in your heatproof container.
  • Allow the tabs to soak until they fully disintegrate.
  • Heat until the solution boils and continue until the solution becomes fully transparent.
  • Pour the agarose solution into the prepared BanditTM mold.
  • Allow gel to solidify completely.
  • Remove the comb, comb supports, and ElectrodamsTM by pulling firmly upwards.
  • Insert the Electrodams.
  • Connect the electrodes.
  • Add 30 ml of 1X TBE electrophoresis buffer.
  • Use a micropipette to load samples into the wells of your gel according the specific instructions in the laboratory guide for the specific dye electrophoresis Learning Lab you are using.
  • Connect the power cords.
  • Conduct electrophoresis for 15-25 minutes.
